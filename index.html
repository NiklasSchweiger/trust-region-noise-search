Trust-Region Noise Search for Black-Box Alignment of Diffusion and Flow Models

Niklas Schweiger1, Karnik Ram1, Daniel Cremers1
1School of Computation, Information and Technology, TU Munich
Paper (Coming Soon) Code

TRS finds better outputs from generative models by searching over the noise — no retraining, no gradients through the model.

TL;DR

Pretrained diffusion and flow models are powerful, but often fall short of specific requirements — generating molecules with precise properties, or images that faithfully follow complex prompts. Existing inference-time alignment approaches either backpropagate through the entire generative process (expensive, prone to manifold drift) or rely on sequential filtering (needs many reward calls or accurate value estimates).

TRS takes a different route: it treats both the generative model and the reward as black boxes and only searches over the source noise. Inspired by Bayesian optimization, it maintains multiple “trust regions” in noise space that adaptively expand when they find good candidates and shift away from underperforming areas — balancing exploration and exploitation under strict evaluation budgets.

Key highlights

  • Model- and reward-agnostic: works with any generative architecture and any reward, including expensive, noisy, or non-differentiable ones
  • Stays on the data manifold — no gradient drift, stable even with many optimization steps
  • Evaluated on text-to-image (SD1.5, SDXL), molecule design, and protein design
  • Outperforms existing noise-optimization and even full trajectory-optimization baselines under identical compute budgets
  • Simple algorithm with minimal hyperparameter tuning across all tasks

Optimizing the noise samples of diffusion and flow models is an increasingly popular approach to align these models to target rewards at inference time. However, we observe that these approaches are usually restricted to differentiable or cheap reward functions, the formulation of the underlying pre-trained generative model, or are memory/compute inefficient. We instead propose a simple trust-region based search algorithm (TRS) which treats the pre-trained generative and reward models as a black-box and only optimizes the source noise. Our approach achieves a good balance between global exploration and local exploitation, and is versatile and easily adaptable to various generative settings with minimal hyperparameter tuning. We evaluate TRS across text-to-image, molecule and protein design tasks, and obtain significantly improved output samples over the base generative models and other inference-time alignment approaches which optimize the source noise sample, or even the entire reverse-time sampling noise trajectories in the case of diffusion models.

How It Works

A generative model maps a noise vector to an output — an image, a molecule, a protein. The mapping is deterministic: change the noise and you change the output. Some noise inputs lead to outputs that score much higher on a given reward. TRS finds them.

Warm-up. TRS starts by sampling a batch of random noise vectors from the model's prior, evaluating all of them, and selecting the top-k as initial “trust-region centers” — the most promising starting points in noise space.

Search. Around each center, TRS maintains a hypercubic trust region and proposes new candidates by applying masked perturbations: each candidate modifies a random subset of the noise dimensions within the region. All candidates across regions are evaluated in parallel, then the regions adapt: a region that finds improvements expands; one that stalls contracts. Crucially, after each iteration all regions are re-centered on the globally best-k noise vectors seen so far — this naturally shifts the algorithm from broad exploration early on toward focused exploitation of the most promising areas.

Why it works. Because TRS only modifies the source noise and never intervenes with the model internals, generated samples remain on the learned data manifold — avoiding the drift problems of gradient-based methods. The trust-region mechanism, inspired by Bayesian optimization, provides structured control over the search scale without needing surrogate models, which we find do not help in the highly non-linear noise spaces of generative models.
